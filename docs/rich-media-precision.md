# Rich Media Reply with Per-Card Precision

> An AI response that includes a photo of a water filter when the customer asked "Where is the water source?" looks plausible. An AI response that includes the same photo of a water filter when the customer just said "wrong send" is bizarre. We hit the second case in production, traced it back, and rebuilt the matching logic to prevent it. Here's the design that came out of that incident.

## What Rich Media Reply Means in Chungtair

When a customer asks a question, the answer Chungtair AI generates may include not just text but also images and a URL — all delivered as a multi-message bundle on whichever platform (LINE / Facebook Messenger / Instagram) the customer is on. The text is generated by the LLM from retrieved knowledge; the images and URL are pulled directly from the matched knowledge base entries.

The wins are obvious. The customer doesn't need to navigate to another page to see a product photo. The merchant doesn't need to copy-paste image links into an answer. A single bot reply does the work of a multi-step interaction.

But there's a precision problem that took us a real bug to discover.

## The Bug That Forced This Redesign

A customer messaged a merchant's LINE bot with the simple sentence "wrong send" — the kind of casual message a customer might type if they sent something by mistake and wanted to cancel it. The merchant's knowledge base had no entry covering this case. There was no good answer to retrieve.

What the retrieval system found instead was a marginal match: an entry titled "Water Source Location" that scored low on similarity (the only commonality being that both involved water indirectly), but high enough to make the top three retrieved entries. The "Water Source Location" entry happened to have a photo of a water filter attached.

Under the older logic, when the LLM generated its (already weak) text response, the system collected images from all top-three retrieved entries to send alongside. The customer received a confused text message plus a completely unrelated photo of a water filter.

The text was bad. The unrelated photo made it worse.

## The Redesign — Per-Card Precision Check

The fix is conceptually simple: **each retrieved entry must independently clear a precision threshold before it is allowed to contribute its attached media to the response.**

Before the fix, top-ranked entries collectively contributed all their media. After the fix:

- Each entry's individual similarity score is checked against the same minimum precision threshold the merchant configured for the entire RAG layer.
- Entries scoring above the threshold contribute their image and URL.
- Entries scoring below the threshold contribute neither — even if they were included in the top three for context purposes.

The threshold is the same one a merchant already uses to control the overall RAG layer's strictness, so no new dial was introduced. It's the existing precision dial, now applied per knowledge card rather than as a single gate on the whole batch.

## Why Not Just Raise the Overall Threshold?

Raising the threshold for the whole RAG layer would solve the image problem by causing the system to fall through to fallback more often. But that throws away the genuinely useful capability of letting the LLM synthesize an answer from multiple weak signals when no single strong match exists — for example, when a customer's question can only be answered by combining two related entries that each cover half of it.

The per-card precision check keeps that synthesis capability for text generation while restricting media attachment to confident matches only. Different decisions for different concerns.

## What This Means in Practice

For the merchant: configuring the precision threshold now does double duty — it controls both when the system falls through to fallback, and which retrieved entries are allowed to attach images. One dial, two effects, both aligned.

For the customer: when they get a photo with their answer, it's almost always the right photo. When the system isn't confident enough, it sends just text — which is far better than sending the wrong image.

For us as engineers: a real customer-facing bug led to a design improvement that strengthened a core capability. We chose to document it rather than quietly patch it. Edge cases that get hit in production deserve to be remembered.
